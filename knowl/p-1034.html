The equation \(r_1\vec x_1+r_2\vec x_2+\cdots+r_n\vec x_n=\vec w\) is the same as the matrix equation

\begin{equation*} X \begin{bmatrix} r_1\\r_2\\ \vdots\\r_n \end{bmatrix} = \vec w \end{equation*}

and so, setting \(\vec w=\vec0\text{,}\) we see that \(\mathcal{X}\) is linearly independent if and only if \(X \begin{bmatrix} r_1\\ \vdots\\r_n \end{bmatrix} =\vec 0\) implies \(\begin{bmatrix} r_1\\ \vdots\\r_n \end{bmatrix} =\vec0\text{.}\) By TheoremÂ 2.11.2, this is equivalent to \(X\) being nonsingular.
