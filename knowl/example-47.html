Example 4.10.4. The projection of a point to a line in \(\R^2\) revisited.

Consider the computation of the projection of a point \((x,y)\) onto the line \(y=2x\text{.}\) The strategy is to first use the line as one axis for a basis. To that end we take a nonzero point on the line: \(\vec u_1=(1,2)\) as the first basis element. Next, we want a second basis element orthogonal to \(\vec u_1\) so that the parallelogram rule will be applied to a rectangle. An easy choice is \((-2,1)\) (why?), so the basis is \(B=\{(1,2),(-2,1)\}\text{.}\) The coordinates \((r_1,r_2)\) of \((x,y)\) with respect to \(B\) are found by solving

\begin{equation*} r_1(1,2)+r_2(-2,1)=(x,y)\text{.} \end{equation*}

This is routine and, in particular, gives us \(r_1=\frac15(x+2y)\text{.}\) Note that \(r_1\vec u_1=\frac15(x+2y)(1,2)=\frac15(x+2y,2x+4y)\) is our desired point. Notice that we could evaluate \(r_2\text{,}\) but we don't need to because of our choice of basis.
