Finding the plane containing three points: method 1.

The general equation of the plane is

\begin{equation*} ax+by+cz+d=0 \end{equation*}

for some \(a\text{,}\) \(b\) and \(c\) not all zero. Substitute the three points in the equation to get three equations in four unknowns (the unknowns are \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) \(c\) and \(d\)).

Example of this method: Let the three points be \((1,1,3)\text{,}\) \((1,0,2)\) and \((2,1,1)\text{.}\) Each point gives an equation: \(\begin{align} a+b+3c+d\amp=0 \tag{from the point \((1,1,3)\)}\\ a\phantom{+2b}+2c+d\amp=0\tag{from the point \((1,0,2)\)}\\ 2a+b+c+d\amp=0\tag{from the point \((2,1,1)\)} \end{align} \text{.}\) The augmented matrix for this system is

\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1\amp1\amp3\amp1\amp0\\ 1\amp0\amp2\amp1\amp0\\ 2\amp1\amp1\amp1\amp0 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}

which has the reduced row echelon form:

\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1\amp0\amp0\amp\frac12\amp0\\ 0\amp1\amp0\amp-\frac14\amp0\\ 0\amp0\amp1\amp\frac14\amp0 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}

This means that \(d\) is a free variable, \(c=-\frac14 d\text{,}\) \(b=\frac14 d\) and \(a=-\frac12 d\text{.}\) This makes the equation of the plane

\begin{equation*} ax+by+cz+d=-\frac12 d x+\frac14 d y-\frac14 d z +d =0 \end{equation*}

Dividing the equation by \(d\) gives the equation

\begin{equation*} -\frac12 x+\frac14 y-\frac14 z +1 =0 \end{equation*}

which is an equation of the plane. Multiplying by \(-4\) can make the equation a bit cleaner:

\begin{equation*} 2x-y+z-4=0 \end{equation*}

As a check, for \((1,1,3)\) we have \(2x-y+z-4=2-1+3-4=0\text{,}\) for \((1,0,2)\) we have \(2x-y+z-4=2-0+2-4=0\) and for \((2,1,1)\) we have \(2x-y+z-4=4-1+1-4=0\text{.}\)
